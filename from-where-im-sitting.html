From Where I'm Sitting

On misinformation, incentives, and the uncomfortable possibility that we're not the audience — we're the metric.

July 23, 2026 · Souhaib Ben Farhat

In 2018, three MIT researchers published the largest study ever done on how news spreads: 126,000 stories shared across four and a half million tweets. False stories were seventy percent more likely to be shared and reached fifteen hundred people six times faster than true ones. It wasn't bots. They stripped the bot traffic out and the gap held.

The usual reading is that lies outrun truth. I've started to doubt that's the right lesson, and what follows is me thinking out loud rather than anything I'd call settled.

Look at what predicts whether something spreads. A 2021 study analysed 2.7 million posts from news outlets and members of Congress and found that posts attacking the political out-group were shared roughly twice as often as posts about their own side, and that out-group animosity beat every other predictor tested, including emotional language itself.

That makes me think the variable was never true or false. It looks more like how hard the content pushes people apart.

If that's right, then a true story engineered to polarise should travel about as far as a fake one, and falsehoods only win more often because they're easier to build that way. The formula wouldn't be "be wrong." It would be "be against something."

I work on systems that optimise for a metric, and the thing you learn quickly is that a system gives you exactly what you measured, never what you meant. Optimise for attention and I'd expect division, not because anyone chose division but because it scores highest and truth isn't a term in the equation.

There's a second piece I find harder to shake. Corrections don't seem to travel as far as the claims they correct. The reward for being loud arrives immediately, while the cost of being wrong arrives late, quiet, and to a much smaller room, long after the attention was already sold. If that holds, being wrong isn't really a cost. It's a deferred one, heavily discounted.

And if being wrong is cheap enough, I'd expect the working definition of "right" to drift. Not toward accurate. Toward not yet caught.

I can't see inside anyone's newsroom, and I'm wary of theories that explain everything. But here's the pattern I keep noticing. When an outlet reverses a position it defended for years, the reversal seems to track the audience more closely than it tracks the evidence. The evidence was often available the whole time. What changed was the size of the crowd.

There may be a further step. In a system that counts attention, contradicting yourself pays twice: once for the claim, once for the reversal. Consistency pays once, and it pays less each time, because consistency isn't novel and novelty is what travels. Which would make a reversal something other than damage to be managed.

In October 1925, Gandhi published a list in his newspaper Young India, sent to him by an English clergyman. Seven social sins. The first was politics without principle.

A century early, and still the cleanest name I've found for it. Principle is the constraint you keep when it costs you something. Take it away and you don't get villains, you get optimisation — support that lasts exactly as long as it pays.

None of this requires anyone to be lying, which is the part I find most uncomfortable.

And I don't think the platforms are really the ones choosing. Every share, every furious quote-tweet, every click on the headline we already knew was bait, is a vote counted at full weight.

Maybe I have this wrong. But from where I'm sitting, we're not the audience. We're the metric.
