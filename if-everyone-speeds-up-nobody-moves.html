If Everyone Speeds Up, Nobody Moves

Notes on what AI actually moved, and what it only appeared to.

July 22, 2026 · Souhaib Ben Farhat

Imagine every car on the road gains twenty kilometres an hour at the same moment. You included.

You'd arrive sooner, but you wouldn't overtake anyone. Your position in traffic would be exactly what it was. The whole road moved, so nothing between the cars changed.

That's the first thing I think gets missed about AI. A capability everyone receives at once isn't an advantage to anyone. It raises the whole road.

Which is why the shrug from experienced engineers makes sense to me. From inside the traffic, faster traffic still feels like traffic.

Except for the ones who were parked

The metaphor breaks in one specific place, and that's where everything interesting lives.

Not everyone was moving. Some people were stopped — held up by one particular thing. And removing a constraint only helps whoever that constraint was actually binding.

If your bottleneck was capital, or customers, or knowing which problem deserved solving, almost nothing moved for you. Your obstacle sits exactly where it sat.

But if your only obstacle was execution — you understood the problem, you could see the shape of the answer, you simply had no way to build it — the road didn't speed up for you. The barrier disappeared.

That's why the reactions are so uneven. The engineer says it's just faster. The person who spent five years unable to build the thing in their head says the ground moved. Same tool, different binding constraint.

Abundance isn't mastery

The abundance of cars didn't make everyone a race driver.

Access raises the floor. It doesn't raise the ceiling. Anyone can produce working software now, the way anyone can drive — which is not the same as producing good software, any more than owning a car makes you quick around a track.

But cars did change something, and it wasn't skill. They ended the horse trades. They created mechanics, truckers, logistics, road engineers, whole systems for managing traffic. The abundance didn't equalise ability. It relocated the work.

So the question was never whether the work disappears. It's where it goes.

It went to everything that isn't the code

If building is cheap, whatever you build can be rebuilt. Feature parity arrives in weeks. The code stops being the thing that sets you apart.

Though I'd put that more precisely: code was never really what set you apart. It only looked that way while building was expensive. When something is hard, the ability to do it resembles an advantage. Take the difficulty away and you finally see what was underneath the whole time.

What's underneath is unglamorous. Distribution — whether you can reach anyone at all. Trust, which takes years and can't be manufactured on demand. Data that improves as people use it. Being embedded deep enough in someone's workflow that replacing you costs more than keeping you. And taste: knowing which problem deserves the effort in the first place.

Features are copyable. The judgment behind them isn't.

The same shift, further down

Once you notice it, it turns up in odd places.

Coordination, for one. Communication paths grow roughly with the square of team size, and that tax was worth paying when you genuinely needed many hands. If a smaller group now produces what a large one used to, the tax becomes waste. When building gets cheap, coordination becomes the expensive part, and the efficient unit shrinks.

Though splitting only helps if the boundaries are real. Cut a tightly coupled system and you don't get autonomy — you get the same coordination, now running over a network and a shared calendar. Knowing where the seams actually are is architecture. Judgment again, wearing different clothes.

Hiring shows it too. Interviews still ask whether you can recall a definition, at a moment when recall is the cheapest thing in the building. Not that the fundamentals stopped mattering — you can't review code you don't understand, and unreviewed output isn't finished work, it's volume. But there's a difference between reciting what a closure is and spotting the bug where one captured the wrong variable. One tests memory. The other tests what we still actually need.

The question I can't settle

Two things above pull against each other, and I haven't worked out which one wins.

The small-unit argument says the advantage goes to whoever is nimble. Less coordination overhead, faster conversion of the speedup into real output, no legacy dragging behind. A handful of people can now build what used to need a floor of them, so the newcomers should be eating everyone.

But look again at what's left to compete on — distribution, trust, data, being embedded in someone's workflow. Those are precisely the things a large incumbent already has and a newcomer doesn't. And if code has stopped being what sets you apart, then the one thing a small company could reliably win on — building something better — just got commoditised. The game moves to a board where the incumbent already owns most of the pieces.

Which is the question I keep circling: does a general amplifier lift everyone equally, or does it mostly lift whoever was already strong?

Because if what it removes is the execution barrier, and a newcomer's real barrier was never execution but reach, then AI solved a problem they didn't have and handed a gift to the company that did.

I don't think it resolves cleanly. Large organisations may well find their code got ten times faster while their approvals, their compliance and their internal politics didn't — and the whole gain evaporates in that gap. Advantage on paper isn't advantage in practice, and incumbents have a long history of failing to convert exactly this kind of edge.

Maybe the honest answer is that amplifiers democratise early and concentrate late, and the only real question is which part of that curve we're standing on.

Ask for an opinion, not an answer

I've noticed something while arguing about all of this: the people who sound most certain are usually the ones I trust least.

That isn't a personality flaw on their part. It's a signal. This is genuinely new, the evidence is thin, and anyone confidently describing how it ends is usually selling something — or hasn't yet looked hard enough at their own assumptions to find the holes. Forecasting research keeps landing on the same result: people who hold one big confident idea predict worse than people who hold several loosely and keep revising them.

So I've stopped asking anyone for the answer. I ask what they think, and why.

The difference matters more than it sounds. An answer asks you to trust it. An opinion shows its work — which means you can inspect the reasoning, keep the parts that hold, and throw out the parts that don't. You can argue with an opinion. You can only accept or reject an answer.

And there's something specific to this moment in it. Confident, fluent, authoritative-sounding answers just became free to produce. Anyone can generate one now, on any subject, in seconds. What hasn't become cheap is someone telling you honestly how sure they actually are.

Which makes "I don't know, but here's how I'm thinking about it" worth more than it has ever been.

Relevance is never free

None of this settles into a comfortable position, and I think that's the real discomfort under the whole AI conversation.

You don't earn a place in this field once. You re-earn it. The floor keeps rising, and standing still on a rising floor feels like movement until you look around.

That sounds harsher than I mean it. It's simply what this work has always been. It just used to move slowly enough to ignore.

Thirty seconds

One last thing, because it's the part that steadies me.

Compress the entire history of the universe into a single year. All of recorded history takes about eleven seconds. Everything since agriculture — every city, every language, every empire — fits into the last thirty. A whole human life is roughly one blink.

We have been looking at any of this for thirty seconds.

So when someone tells me the problems are running out, or that the interesting work is behind us, I notice they're extrapolating from thirty seconds of data. Every answer we've found has opened questions we didn't previously know how to ask. You can finish a thing. I don't think you can finish the work.

We're one blink in, and still early.
