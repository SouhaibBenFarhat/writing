The Senior Engineer's Paradox

The more senior the room, the fewer facts are left to decide anything — so past a certain level, engineering stops being a technical problem and becomes a social one.

July 23, 2026 · Souhaib Ben Farhat

You get promoted for being right. Then one day you're in a room where everyone is right, and nothing moves. Nobody warned you that the ladder you climbed was carrying you out of the part of the job that has answers — and into the part that doesn't.

I. Seniority is a subtraction machine

The questions with answers get filtered out first

Junior work has right answers. Does it compile? Does the test pass? Is this loop O(n) or O(n²)? Did it null-pointer on the empty list? Facts settle all of it. Nobody argues with a crash — you read the trace, you fix the line, the argument is over before it starts.

As you climb, those questions get answered and filtered away. What's left at the top is the residue: should this be a monolith or three services? REST or GraphQL? where does this module's boundary go? is this abstraction paying for itself, or is it just clever? Read them again — none has a stack trace waiting to end the debate.

That's the trick nobody tells you. Seniority doesn't graduate you into harder facts. It graduates you out of facts entirely, into taste. The work that's left over after everything countable has been settled is precisely the work that can't be.

A stack trace ends an argument. A design review starts one. The difference is the whole essay.

II. A room where everyone is right

Six defensible tastes and no stack trace to break the tie

Put six seniors in a room and watch them disagree — not because one of them is wrong, but because none of them is. Six people, six defensible tastes, and no crash to end it. Every captain has a hand on the wheel and every captain is steering true; the ship still doesn't move.

This is the paradox in one sentence: the expertise that makes them senior is exactly what makes them impossible to reconcile by argument. Add more seniors and you don't converge faster — you diverge harder. Each new expert brings one more fully-loaded, fully-defensible opinion to a question that was never going to be settled by loading more opinions into it.

Seen in the wild — the 90-minute architecture review The reactor gets nodded through; the bike shed gets a holy war. The genuinely hard, high-stakes decision passes fast because few in the room have the facts to challenge it. Then everyone spends an hour on the trivial-but-visible thing — the folder name, the config format — because that one, everyone can have a taste about. The triviality isn't a bug in the meeting. It's where the taste has room to spread. (Parkinson's Law of Triviality, 1957: a committee waves through a nuclear reactor in minutes, then burns 45 on the color of the bike shed — because everyone owns enough facts to fight about the shed, and nobody owns enough to fight about the reactor.)

It's why the two-pizza team decides and the twelve-person one deliberates. Beyond a certain headcount you're not adding horsepower, you're adding vetoes — each one reasonable, each one true, and collectively enough to stall the whole thing in the harbor.

A team's ability to decide can go down as its average seniority goes up.

III. Being right was never the lever

You can be provably correct and still lose the team

Here's the move that breaks careers. If taste can't be settled by facts, then winning the argument settles nothing. You can be provably, gloriously correct and still walk out having lost the room — because on a taste question there was no "correct" to win, only people to bring along, and you spent your energy proving instead of persuading.

The senior who mistakes every taste-question for a fact-question becomes the most expensive person on the team. They wield correctness as a weapon. They win the design review and lose the three people who now quietly route around them. Every org has one: brilliant, usually right, and slowly making everyone around them worse.

The archetype — "right, but alone" The strongest teams learned to name this and refuse it: no brilliant jerks. Not because the brilliance isn't real — it is — but because the cost of one person treating collaboration as a debate to be won is measured in everyone else's output, and that bill is always larger than the genius. Being right becomes a liability the moment it's the only tool you own. (In 2018 Linus Torvalds stepped away from Linux maintainership to work on how he talks to people. Decades of being technically right had a body count of burned-out contributors. Right, it turned out, was not the same as effective.)

At this altitude the only currency that spends is influence: getting people to want your idea, not proving they must accept it. And those are different skills — one you were rewarded for the whole way up, the other nobody taught you, because for nine years you didn't need it.

IV. The master skill

Know which half of the room you're standing in

Every decision on the table is one of two kinds, and the entire skill is telling them apart in real time:

- Fact. There is a right answer. This is a security hole, a data-loss bug, a race condition. Challenge hard, bring the evidence, and do not let it slide to keep the peace. The stack trace is on your side; use it. - Taste. There is only agree or disagree. This is the folder layout, the naming, the shape of an abstraction reasonable people will never fully agree on. Make your case once — then let someone own it and move. Dying on this hill costs you the credibility you'll need on the next hill that actually has a summit.

Great engineers switch modes in seconds. Mediocre ones run every question through the fact protocol — treating a taste disagreement as a thing to be proven — and turn a one-hour design review into a war of attrition nobody wins. The tell: is this countable, or is this opinion? Ask it before you open your mouth. Half the fights on a senior team are just two people who never checked which half they were in.

Two protocols worth internalizing - Chesterton's Fence — don't tear down what you don't yet understand. - Disagree & commit — the protocol for taste: argue hard, then row the boat even if you lost the vote.

V. The part that stings

If the only way you can move the team is by being right, you've already lost the job

Leadership at this level is carrying ideas by influence, not authority — and the best in the craft built a whole culture around admitting it. The design doc, the RFC, the proposal circulated for comment: these aren't bureaucracy. They're the senior engineer saying, out loud, I am going to win this on persuasion, in the open, and I am inviting you to try to break it. The strongest technical leaders you know move through documents and conversations, not commits. Their leverage stopped being their own keyboard a long time ago.

RFC = Request For Comments. The dominant format for serious technical decisions is literally named after asking to be challenged. That's not humility theater — it's the influence protocol written down.

So here's the one that stings. If the only way you can move the team is by being the loudest fact in the room — if you can't get people to pick up an idea and make it their own — you're not leading. You're just winning. And winning is failing the actual job, because the job stopped being "be right" around the same promotion that carried you out of the land of stack traces.

The paradox has a cruel symmetry to it: the skill that got you here is the one you now have to put down. Not because it stopped being valuable — a senior who can't tell a real bug from a taste is useless — but because it stopped being enough. You were promoted into the unsolvable half of the work, and nobody hands you a new manual at the door.

The best teams aren't the ones with the most right answers. They're the ones who know when the right answer stopped mattering.
